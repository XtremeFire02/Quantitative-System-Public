\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

This document records Phase 3 of the BTCUSDT perpetual futures signal
research programme.  It is a self-contained technical record of the
design, derivation, testing, and position-level backtesting of three
candidate signals.  The core results are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{P3 (OI-Price Divergence, DD regime) is a statistically
        independent second alpha.}  The DD regime --- simultaneous
        price decline and open-interest contraction --- predicts positive
        24-hour returns with OOS Sharpe $+5.18$ on 57 exclusive trades
        (trades where N3 was not simultaneously active), block-bootstrap
        $p = 0.007$.  P3 is deployed to shadow paper trading.

  \item \textbf{P1 (Volatility Risk Premium) is conditionally interesting
        but not independent of N3.}  VRP$^z$ achieves statistically
        significant IC at 48--72h ($p_{\mathrm{boot}} < 0.025$), but when
        N3 is excluded from the DVOL $51$--$60$ zone, P1 Sharpe turns
        negative ($-0.50$, $p = 0.732$).  P1 does not add independent alpha.

  \item \textbf{P2 (Taker Aggression Ratio) is killed.}  IC is near zero
        and unstable at all horizons.  The market is in approximate taker
        equilibrium at daily frequency, leaving no directional information
        in aggregate taker flow.
\end{enumerate}

Prior exploratory work (unreported) found that microstructure mean-reversion
signals are below maker-cost breakeven after 2023.  Report A1 validated N3
(Deribit 30-day DVOL $z$-score, DVOL $\geq \delta$ volatility-regime filter) with OOS
Sharpe $+2.95$ over 863 days.  Phase 3 departs entirely from these earlier
signal families.

The report proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:market} introduces

Section~\ref{sec:stats} summarises the statistical framework.
Sections~\ref{sec:p1}--\ref{sec:p3} present each signal.

\section{Market Background}
\label{sec:market}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Open Interest in Perpetual Futures}
\label{sec:oi}

\begin{definition}[Open Interest]
  The \emph{open interest} (OI) of a derivative contract is the total
  number of outstanding (open) contracts that have not yet been closed,
  settled, or exercised.  Each contract represents one long position and
  one short position.  OI is measured in units of contracts (or equivalently,
  in BTC notional at the contract size).
\end{definition}

Unlike volume, which counts every transaction, OI counts only the
\emph{currently live} positions. The key accounting identity is:

\begin{equation}
  \Delta\OI_t = N_t^{\text{open}} - N_t^{\text{close}}
\end{equation}

where $N_t^{\text{open}}$ is the number of new contracts created and
$N_t^{\text{close}}$ is the number of contracts terminated in period $t$.

Three types of transactions update OI differently:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{New position} (neither party held the contract): one new
        long and one new short are created. $\Delta\OI = +1$.
  \item \textbf{Both sides closing} (both parties offsetting existing
        positions): OI decreases by 1. $\Delta\OI = -1$.
  \item \textbf{Transfer} (one side opens, one side closes): no net change
        to OI. $\Delta\OI = 0$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Economic interpretation of OI changes.}
The sign of $\Delta\OI$ in conjunction with the price direction carries
information about the nature of the move:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccp{9cm}}
\toprule
$\Delta P$ & $\Delta\OI$ & Economic interpretation \\
\midrule
$>0$ & $>0$ & Price rising, new longs entering. Capital-backed uptrend; existing shorts covering while new longs open. \\
$>0$ & $<0$ & Price rising, positions closing. Short squeeze: shorts are forced to buy to close; fewer net participants remain after the move. \\
$<0$ & $>0$ & Price falling, new positions opening. New shorts entering; conviction-driven downtrend. \\
$<0$ & $<0$ & Price falling, positions closing. Long liquidations: longs are forced to sell, removing supply. After the flush, the pressure reverses. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

This four-regime taxonomy is the foundation of Signal P3
(Section~\ref{sec:p3}).

\paragraph{Why OI matters for crypto perpetuals.}
Crypto perpetuals are levered instruments. The maximum leverage on
Binance BTCUSDT is $125\times$, though typical retail participants
use $3$--$10\times$.  When a levered long position's margin falls below
the maintenance requirement, the exchange's liquidation engine forcibly
closes it: it sells the position to the market.

A period of forced long liquidations ($\Delta P < 0$, $\Delta\OI < 0$)
implies that:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item The sellers are not discretionary sellers making an informed bet
        on future price direction; they are \emph{forced} sellers.
  \item The supply of forced sellers is bounded: once all over-levered
        longs are liquidated, forced selling stops.
  \item After the liquidation cascade clears, the market faces a supply
        vacuum: fewer longs remain (OI is lower), the funding rate
        typically resets, and price can recover without resistance from
        the eliminated over-leveraged positions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Volatility Risk Premium}
\label{sec:vrp_theory}

\subsubsection{Risk-Neutral versus Physical Measures}

Options are priced under the \emph{risk-neutral measure} $\mathbb{Q}$,
not the physical (real-world) measure $\mathbb{P}$.  These measures
coincide if and only if investors are risk-neutral with respect to the
payoff in question.  For variance, they diverge substantially.

Variance is a \emph{negatively skewed} risk: in bad economic times
(market crashes, financial crises), realised volatility spikes.  An
investor who holds assets that pay off \emph{badly} precisely when
volatility is high will demand compensation for bearing this risk.
In the language of asset pricing, negative covariance between an
investor's marginal utility and the variance of the portfolio earns a
positive risk premium.

\begin{definition}[Variance Risk Premium]
  The \emph{variance risk premium} (VRP) is the spread between the
  risk-neutral expectation of future variance (implied by option prices)
  and the physical expectation of future variance (the expected
  realised variance):
  \begin{equation}
    \VRP_t = \E^{\mathbb{Q}}_t[\sigma^2_{t, t+T}]
             - \E^{\mathbb{P}}_t[\sigma^2_{t, t+T}]
    \label{eq:vrp_theoretical}
  \end{equation}
  Under standard regularity conditions (no-arbitrage, risk-aversion),
  $\VRP_t > 0$: the implied variance exceeds the expected realised variance.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Empirical implementation.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:vrp_theoretical} involves latent objects
($\E^{\mathbb{Q}}$ and $\E^{\mathbb{P}}$).  In practice:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item $\E^{\mathbb{Q}}_t[\sigma^2]$ is proxied by $\mathrm{DVOL}_t^2$
        --- the Deribit model-free implied variance annualised (see A1 Section 2.2).
  \item $\E^{\mathbb{P}}_t[\sigma^2]$ is proxied by the 30-day historical
        realised variance, computed from 1-hour log returns.
\end{itemize}

Converting to annualised percentage units gives:
\begin{equation}
  \VRP_t = \DVOL_t - \sigma^{\mathrm{rv}}_t
  \label{eq:vrp_empirical}
\end{equation}
where $\DVOL_t$ is in \% and $\sigma^{\mathrm{rv}}_t$ is the 30-day
annualised realised volatility in \%.

\subsubsection{Why VRP Predicts Future Returns}

In equity markets, Bollerslev et al.\ (2009) show that VRP
predicts excess returns: a high VRP means investors are collectively
over-paying for variance protection, which tends to be corrected as
uncertainty resolves and implied volatility mean-reverts.  The economic
channel is:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Fear is elevated: investors pay up for downside protection.
        DVOL rises above realised vol.
  \item The fear resolves (the triggering event passes, no crash occurs).
  \item IV mean-reverts, option sellers collect the premium, and spot
        prices recover as hedging demand fades.
\end{enumerate}

The sign of the predictive relationship is thus \emph{positive}: a high
VRP predicts positive future returns.  This is the same mechanism as
N3's fear-resolution channel (A1, Section 2.3), but VRP captures an
additional dimension: not just whether fear is elevated relative to
history ($z$-score), but whether it is elevated relative to the
\emph{option market's own recent realised risk}.

\subsection{Order Flow and Taker Aggression}
\label{sec:taker_theory}

\subsubsection{Makers, Takers, and the Limit Order Book}

A \emph{limit order book} is the central mechanism for price discovery
in exchange-traded markets.  Participants submit two types of orders:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Limit orders (makers):} Orders specifying a price at
        which the participant is willing to trade. They rest in the book
        as \emph{passive} quotes. The participant earns the bid-ask
        spread (approximately) and pays a lower exchange fee in exchange
        for providing liquidity.

  \item \textbf{Market orders / aggressive limit orders (takers):}
        Orders that execute immediately against the best available
        resting quote. The participant pays the spread and a higher fee
        in exchange for certainty of execution.
\end{itemize}

The convention is that every trade has exactly one maker and one taker.
\emph{Taker-buy} volume is the volume of contracts where the buyer was
the aggressor (submitted a market buy or lifted the ask); taker-sell
volume is analogously defined.

\subsubsection{The Order Flow Imbalance Hypothesis}

A high proportion of taker-buy volume signals that \emph{demand is
aggressive}: buyers are sufficiently eager to pay the ask price rather
than wait. The market microstructure hypothesis (Kyle, 1985; Glosten \& Milgrom, 1985)
is that informed traders are more likely to be takers:
they have time-sensitive information and cannot wait for passive fills.

The \emph{order flow imbalance} (OFI) is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{OFI}_t = \frac{V^{\text{taker-buy}}_t - V^{\text{taker-sell}}_t}
                        {V^{\text{taker-buy}}_t + V^{\text{taker-sell}}_t}
  \in [-1, +1]
\end{equation}

This equals $2\phi_t - 1$ where $\phi_t = V^{\text{buy}}_t / V_t$ is
the taker-buy fraction.  Signal P2 uses $\phi_t$ directly (the TAR).

At intraday frequencies (seconds to minutes), taker imbalance has been
shown to predict short-term price impact in equity markets (Cont et al., 2014).  At the \emph{daily} frequency, the signal is expected
to weaken substantially because:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Informed taker flow occurs on timescales of seconds to minutes,
        not hours; summing over a full day averages away any signal.
  \item At the daily frequency, buy volume equals sell volume to within
        small rounding errors by construction (every buy has a seller),
        so $\phi_t \approx 0.50$ almost always.
  \item Any residual imbalance reflects the \emph{mixture} of many
        distinct informed and uninformed orders, which are mean-zero
        in aggregate under market efficiency at long timescales.
\end{enumerate}

Phase 3 tests whether the daily TAR can nonetheless predict 24--72h
ahead, despite this theoretical expectation.

\section{Statistical Framework}
\label{sec:stats}
%% ============================================================

This section derives the statistical tools used throughout Phase 3 from
first principles.  The treatment is self-contained.

\subsection{Log-Returns as Prediction Targets}

For a price series $\{C_t\}$, the \emph{simple return} over a horizon $h$ is
$\tilde{r}_t^{(h)} = C_{t+h}/C_t - 1$.  The \emph{log-return} is:
\[
  r_t^{(h)} = \log\frac{C_{t+h}}{C_t} = \log C_{t+h} - \log C_t
\]
Log-returns are preferred for three reasons:

\paragraph{Temporal additivity.}
Log-returns over sub-periods add exactly: $r_{t,t+h+k} = r_{t,t+h} + r_{t+h,t+h+k}$.
Simple returns compound multiplicatively, requiring chain multiplication.
This additivity is essential for equity curve construction: the log-equity
curve is the cumulative sum of log-returns.

\paragraph{Symmetry.}
A simple return of $+50\%$ is not cancelled by a subsequent $-50\%$:
$1.5 \times 0.5 = 0.75 \neq 1$.  Log-returns are symmetric: $+\log 1.5$
is exactly cancelled by $-\log 1.5$.  This symmetry makes long and short
positions analytically equivalent, since a short log-return is $-r_t^{(h)}$.

\paragraph{Cost comparability.}
Transaction costs are approximately constant fractions of notional regardless
of direction.  A round-trip cost $c_{\text{RT}}$ is subtracted uniformly from
$r_t^{(h)}$ regardless of its sign or magnitude.  This is exact in log-return
space and approximate in simple return space.

\paragraph{Basis-point convention.}
All PnL, drawdown, and per-trade figures are stated in \emph{basis points}:
$1\bp = 0.01\% = 10^{-4}$.  A log-return of $0.01$ (1\%) equals $100\bp$.

The 24-hour total return including funding payments is:
\begin{equation}
  \pi_t^{(24h)} = \log\frac{C_{t+1440}}{C_t}
                  - \sum_{k:\,\tau_k \in [t, t+1440]} F_{\tau_k}
\end{equation}
where $F_{\tau_k}$ is the 8-hour funding rate at settlement time $\tau_k$,
and the sum covers the three settlements within the hold window.
Round-trip maker cost: $c_{\mathrm{RT}} = 6\bp$ ($3\bp$ entry, $3\bp$ exit).

\subsection{The Spearman Information Coefficient}
\label{sec:IC}

\begin{definition}[Spearman IC]
  Let $\{(s_t, r_t)\}_{t=1}^n$ be a sample of signal-return pairs.
  Define the rank functions:
  \[
    R_t = \rank(s_t) = \left|\{j : s_j \leq s_t\}\right|, \qquad
    Q_t = \rank(r_t) = \left|\{j : r_j \leq r_t\}\right|
  \]
  (ranks from 1 to $n$; midranks for ties).  The Spearman IC is the Pearson
  correlation applied to the rank vectors:
  \begin{equation}
    \IC(s, r) = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^n (R_t - \bar{R})(Q_t - \bar{Q})}
                     {\sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^n (R_t - \bar{R})^2}
                      \sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^n (Q_t - \bar{Q})^2}}
    \label{eq:spearman}
  \end{equation}
  For continuous distributions with no ties, $\bar{R} = \bar{Q} = (n+1)/2$
  and $\sum (R_t - \bar{R})^2 = n(n^2-1)/12$, giving the familiar formula:
  \[
    \IC(s, r) = 1 - \frac{6\sum_{t=1}^n d_t^2}{n(n^2 - 1)},
    \qquad d_t = R_t - Q_t
  \]
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Why Spearman over Pearson?}
The Pearson correlation $\Cor(s, r)$ is the natural measure of
\emph{linear} predictability.  However:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Log-return distributions are leptokurtic; a few large-return days
        dominate the Pearson correlation.  A single outlier can flip the sign.
  \item The relationship between a signal and $r_t^{(24h)}$ need not be
        linear.  The Spearman IC is invariant to any monotone transformation
        of either variable.
  \item Trading strategies based on signal \emph{rank} (enter when the signal
        is in the top quintile) are naturally assessed by rank correlation,
        not level correlation.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}
  A negative IC is equally informative as a positive IC of the same
  magnitude: the signal should simply be traded on the opposite side.
  We report $|\IC|$ and the sign separately throughout.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Breakeven Information Coefficient}
\label{sec:ICstar}

A statistically significant IC does not imply profitability.  The signal must
generate sufficient expected gross profit to cover round-trip transaction costs.
We now derive the minimum IC required.

\begin{proposition}[Breakeven IC]
  \label{prop:ICstar}
  Consider a trading strategy that enters long when a standardised signal
  $z_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is positive and short when $z_t$ is negative,
  exiting after $h$ bars.  Under the linear-IC model
  \begin{equation}
    r_t^{(h)} = \IC \cdot \sigma_r \cdot z_t + \varepsilon_t, \qquad
    \varepsilon_t \perp z_t,\quad
    \Var(r_t^{(h)}) = \sigma_r^2
    \label{eq:linearIC}
  \end{equation}
  the expected gross profit per trade equals
  $\IC \cdot \sigma_r \cdot \sqrt{2/\pi}$.  The signal is profitable
  against a round-trip cost $c_{\mathrm{RT}}$ if and only if
  \begin{equation}
    |\IC| > \IC^* \;\coloneqq\; \frac{c_{\mathrm{RT}}}{\sigma_r \sqrt{2/\pi}}
    \label{eq:ICstar}
  \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Under model~\eqref{eq:linearIC}, since $z_t \perp \varepsilon_t$:
  \[
    \E[r_t^{(h)} \mid z_t] = \IC \cdot \sigma_r \cdot z_t
  \]
  For a long trade conditional on $z_t > 0$:
  \begin{align*}
    \E[r_t^{(h)} \mid z_t > 0]
    &= \IC \cdot \sigma_r \cdot \E[z_t \mid z_t > 0]
    = \IC \cdot \sigma_r \cdot \frac{\E[z_t \cdot \mathbf{1}_{z_t > 0}]}
                                     {P(z_t > 0)}
  \end{align*}
  For $z_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$:
  \[
    \E[z_t \mid z_t > 0]
    = \frac{\int_0^\infty z\,\phi(z)\,\mathrm{d}z}{\tfrac{1}{2}}
    = 2\int_0^\infty z\,\phi(z)\,\mathrm{d}z
    = 2\left[-\phi(z)\right]_0^\infty
    = 2\phi(0)
    = \sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi}}
  \]
  By symmetry, a short trade ($z_t < 0$) earns $\E[-r_t^{(h)} \mid z_t < 0]
  = \IC \cdot \sigma_r \cdot \sqrt{2/\pi}$ as well.

  Expected net profit per trade equals gross minus round-trip cost:
  \[
    \E[\text{net profit}] = \IC \cdot \sigma_r \sqrt{2/\pi} - c_{\mathrm{RT}}
  \]
  Setting this to zero and solving for IC gives~\eqref{eq:ICstar}.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Threshold entry]
  If the strategy only trades when $|z_t| > c$ for some $c > 0$, the expected
  gross profit per trade is $\IC \cdot \sigma_r \cdot \E[z \mid z > c]$.  Since
  $\E[z \mid z > c] = \phi(c)/[1-\Phi(c)] > \sqrt{2/\pi}$ for all $c > 0$,
  the breakeven IC is \emph{lower} than~\eqref{eq:ICstar}.  The formula as
  stated is conservative: it applies to the strategy that enters on every bar.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Pearson vs.\ Spearman]
  Proposition~\ref{prop:ICstar} is stated for the Pearson IC.  The Spearman
  IC satisfies $\IC_S \approx (6/\pi)\arcsin(\IC_P / 2)$, so
  $\IC_S \approx \IC_P$ for $|\IC| \ll 1$.  Since all empirical ICs in this
  report are in $[-0.20, +0.20]$, the same $\IC^*$ formula is applied.
\end{remark}

For the parameters used throughout Phase 3:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item $c_{\mathrm{RT}} = 6\bp$ (maker: $3\bp$ entry, $3\bp$ exit).
  \item $\sigma_r^{(24h)} \approx 2.135\%$ (empirical OOS volatility).
  \item $\IC^*_{\mathrm{maker}} \approx 0.035$.
\end{itemize}

The IC/IC* ratio gives the ``multiple of breakeven'': a ratio of $3\times$
means the signal generates three times the expected gross profit needed
to cover costs.

\subsection{The Overlapping-Return Inflation Problem}
\label{sec:overlap}

The 1-minute BTCUSDT dataset has $T \approx 527{,}040$ observations in the OOS
2024 window.  If we compute $r_t^{(24\text{h})} = \log(C_{t+1440}/C_t)$
for every $t$ and treat all pairs as independent, we overstate statistical
significance severely.

\paragraph{The autocorrelation structure of overlapping returns.}
Under the assumption that 1-minute log-returns are i.i.d.\ with
variance $\delta^2$, the 24h log-return decomposes as:
\[
  r_t^{(24\text{h})} = \sum_{j=0}^{1439} \Delta_j(t),
  \qquad \Delta_j(t) = \log\frac{C_{t+j+1}}{C_{t+j}}
\]
Two adjacent observations share all but one minute of their window.
More generally, for lag $k \leq 1440$:
\begin{align*}
  \Cov\!\left(r_t^{(24\text{h})}, r_{t+k}^{(24\text{h})}\right)
  &= \Cov\!\left(\sum_{j=0}^{1439}\Delta_j(t),\,
                 \sum_{j=0}^{1439}\Delta_j(t+k)\right)
   = (1440 - k)\,\delta^2
\end{align*}
since the two sums share exactly $1440 - k$ common terms (for $k < 1440$).
The autocorrelation at lag $k$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \Cor\!\left(r_t^{(24\text{h})}, r_{t+k}^{(24\text{h})}\right)
  = \frac{(1440 - k)\,\delta^2}{1440\,\delta^2}
  = 1 - \frac{k}{1440}, \qquad 0 \leq k \leq 1440
  \label{eq:overlap_autocor}
\end{equation}
This is a tent function that linearly decays from 1 at lag 0 to 0 at lag 1440.

\paragraph{Effective sample size.}
For a stationary series with autocorrelation function $\rho_k$, the effective
sample size for variance estimation is approximately:
\[
  n_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx \frac{T}{1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \rho_k}
\]
Substituting~\eqref{eq:overlap_autocor}:
\[
  1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{1440-1}\!\!\left(1 - \frac{k}{1440}\right)
  = 1 + 2 \cdot \frac{1440 \cdot 1439}{2 \cdot 1440}
  = 1 + 1439
  = 1440
\]
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
  n_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx \frac{T}{1440} \approx 366
  \label{eq:neff}
\end{equation}
The effective number of independent 24-hour return observations is
approximately 366 per year --- one per day --- regardless of whether we
compute the return at every 1-minute bar or sample once per day.

\paragraph{Practical implication.}
A $t$-statistic computed from the full $n = 527{,}040$ series is inflated
by a factor of $\sqrt{1440} \approx 38$ relative to the correct statistic
based on $n_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 366$.  \textbf{Solution:} sample every
$1{,}440$ bars ($n \approx 365$ non-overlapping daily observations per OOS
year) and apply the block bootstrap.

\subsection{The Block Bootstrap}
\label{sec:bootstrap}

The block bootstrap (Künsch, 1989; Liu \& Singh, 1992) provides valid inference
for statistics of dependent time series without parametric assumptions about
the dependence structure.

\begin{definition}[Non-Overlapping Block Bootstrap]
  Given a time series $(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ and a statistic $T_n$:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item Choose block length $\ell$ and form $m = \lfloor n/\ell \rfloor$
          non-overlapping blocks $B_j = (X_{(j-1)\ell+1}, \ldots, X_{j\ell})$
          for $j = 1, \ldots, m$.
    \item Draw $m$ blocks $B_{j_1}^*, \ldots, B_{j_m}^*$ uniformly at random
          with replacement from $\{B_1, \ldots, B_m\}$.
    \item Concatenate to form the bootstrap resample
          $X^* = (B_{j_1}^*, \ldots, B_{j_m}^*)$; trim to length $n$.
    \item Compute the bootstrap statistic $T_n^* = T_n(X^*)$.
    \item Repeat $B$ times.  The empirical distribution of
          $\{T_n^{*(b)}\}_{b=1}^B$ approximates the sampling distribution
          of $T_n$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

For inference on $\IC$, the block bootstrap preserves temporal autocorrelation
within blocks (capturing weekly seasonality, funding-cycle effects, and
momentum effects) while permuting the dependence structure across blocks.

\paragraph{Block length selection.}
The block length $\ell$ should exceed the effective autocorrelation decay
length of the signal-return interaction.  For the daily non-overlapping series
used here, a block of $\ell = 21$ days (three calendar weeks) captures
relevant dependence: weekly trading patterns, bi-weekly options gamma pinning
cycles near expiry, and multi-week momentum effects.  (A1 used $\ell = 14$
days; the longer block is adopted here to additionally capture multi-week
momentum autocorrelation present in the OI and VRP signals tested in Phase 3.)

\paragraph{$p$-value computation.}
For a one-sided test of $H_0 : \IC = 0$ against $H_1 : \IC > 0$:
\begin{equation}
  p = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B \mathbf{1}\!\left[\IC^{*(b)} \geq \widehat{\IC}\right]
  \label{eq:pvalue}
\end{equation}
where $B = 2{,}000$ resamples are used throughout (A1 used $B = 1{,}000$;
the larger count reduces Monte Carlo noise in the null distribution tail).
A small $p$ means the observed IC would rarely arise if the true predictive
relationship were zero.  The one-sided form is used throughout Phase 3
because the economic mechanism specifies the predicted sign of every signal
in advance; A1 used a two-sided form as N3's positive sign was a finding
to be verified rather than a prior assumption.

\subsection{Walk-Forward Out-of-Sample Validation}
\label{sec:walkforward}

Standard cross-validation violates temporal causality: using future data to
evaluate a model estimated on the past.  For time-series prediction, the
correct protocol is \emph{walk-forward} validation:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Fix all signal parameters using only the training period
        (2023-01-01 to 2023-12-31).
  \item Evaluate the signal \emph{without any re-fitting} on disjoint
        OOS windows.
  \item Report IC and Sharpe separately for each OOS half-year to
        assess temporal stability.
\end{enumerate}

All signals are evaluated on the following windows:

\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Period & Dates & Role \\
\midrule

OOS 2024-H1  & 2024-01-01 to 2024-06-30 & Out-of-sample \\
OOS 2024-H2  & 2024-07-01 to 2024-12-31 & Out-of-sample \\

\section{P1: Volatility Risk Premium (VRP)}
\label{sec:p1}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Economic Mechanism}

The theoretical basis for VRP as a return predictor follows from the
risk-premium channel described in Section~\ref{sec:vrp_theory}: when
implied volatility substantially exceeds realised volatility, investors
have over-paid for uncertainty protection.  As the uncertainty resolves,
implied volatility mean-reverts toward realised vol, options sellers
collect the premium, and the spot price recovers as hedging demand fades.

In Bitcoin specifically, the BTC variance risk premium is positive on
average (DVOL exceeds realised vol by $4.8$ percentage points), consistent
with the cross-asset VRP literature (Almeida et al., 2024).  The sign
prediction for the IC is therefore \emph{positive}: a higher VRP should
predict higher subsequent returns.

\begin{remark}
  This mechanism is related to N3's fear-resolution hypothesis but
  distinct: N3 measures whether DVOL is above its \emph{own recent
  history} ($z$-score channel), while VRP measures whether DVOL is
  above the \emph{market's own recent realised vol} (risk-premium
  channel).  A signal could be positive in one and zero in the other.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Signal Construction}

\begin{definition}[30-Day Realised Volatility]
  Let $p_\tau$ denote the 1-hour BTC close price.  The 30-day rolling
  realised volatility is:
  \begin{equation}
    \sigma^{\mathrm{rv}}_t = 100\sqrt{365 \times 24} \times
      \mathrm{std}\!\left(\{\log p_{\tau-k} - \log p_{\tau-k-1}\}_{k=1}^{720}\right)
  \end{equation}
  expressed in annualised percentage units, using the same frequency as
  DVOL for direct comparability.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Volatility Risk Premium]
  \begin{align}
    \VRP_t &= \DVOL_t - \sigma^{\mathrm{rv}}_t
      \quad\text{(raw spread, \%pp)} \\
    \VRP^z_t &= \frac{\VRP_t - \mu_{30}(\VRP)}{\sigma_{30}(\VRP)}
      \quad\text{(30-day rolling $z$-score)} \\
    \Delta\VRP_t &= \VRP_t - \VRP_{t-1}
      \quad\text{(day-over-day change)}
  \end{align}
\end{definition}

\section{P2: Taker Aggression Ratio (TAR)}
\label{sec:p2}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Economic Mechanism}

Taker flow is the most direct observable of short-term order-flow
imbalance: when buyers lift asks more aggressively than sellers hit bids,
price pressure is upward.  At the daily frequency, elevated taker buy
fraction could reflect either (a) informed buying that continues
(momentum) or (b) retail over-reach that reverts (contrarian).

Section~\ref{sec:taker_theory} explained why this mechanism is
theoretically expected to \emph{fail} at the daily frequency: informed
taker flow occurs on second-to-minute timescales, and aggregating over
a full trading day washes out the signal.  Phase 3 empirically tests
this expectation.

\subsection{Signal Construction}

\begin{definition}[Taker Aggression Ratio]
  \begin{equation}
    \TAR_t = \frac{\sum_{i \in [t-1440, t]} v^{\mathrm{buy}}_i}
                  {\sum_{i \in [t-1440, t]} v_i}
  \end{equation}
  where $v^{\mathrm{buy}}_i$ is the Binance 1-minute kline field
  \texttt{taker\_buy\_base\_volume} and $v_i$ is total volume.
  The signed order flow imbalance is $\mathrm{OFI}_t = 2\TAR_t - 1$.
  The z-scored variant $\TAR^z_t = (\TAR_t - \mu_{30}) / \sigma_{30}$
  and 5-day momentum $\Delta_5\TAR$ are also tested.
\end{definition}

\section{P3: OI-Price Divergence (OI-PD)}
\label{sec:p3}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Economic Mechanism}

Section~\ref{sec:oi} established that the sign of $(\Delta P, \Delta\OI)$
pairs distinguishes between fundamentally different market processes.  The
key hypothesis tested here is that the \textbf{DD regime} (simultaneous
price decline and OI contraction) represents long-position exhaustion,
generating a predictable mean-reversion impulse:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A downward price move triggers margin calls on levered longs.
  \item Liquidation of longs reduces OI simultaneously with price.
  \item Forced supply is bounded: once over-levered positions are
        cleared, the selling pressure exhausts.
  \item The market recoils upward as new buyers enter at depressed prices,
        unimpeded by the now-liquidated competing longs.
\end{enumerate}

This is the \emph{exhaustion-bounce} mechanism, specific to the leveraged
crypto perpetual market structure.  It contrasts with the DU regime
($\Delta P < 0$, $\Delta\OI > 0$: price falls while new shorts enter),
which may represent informed conviction and continuation.

\begin{remark}
  The key distinction between DD and DU is the \emph{identity of the
  seller}.  In DD: the sellers are forced liquidators (not discretionary).
  In DU: the sellers (new shorts) are making an active directional bet.
  Forced sellers are not informative about future direction; discretionary
  shorts may be.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Signal Construction}

\begin{definition}[OI-Price Divergence Regime]
  Let $\Delta P_t^{24h} = \log(C_t / C_{t-1440})$ and
  $\Delta\OI_t^{24h} = (\OI_t - \OI_{t-1440}) / \OI_{t-1440}$.
  The regime is:
  \begin{equation}
    R_t =
    \begin{cases}
      \mathrm{UU} & \Delta P > 0,\; \Delta\OI > 0 \\
      \mathrm{UD} & \Delta P > 0,\; \Delta\OI \leq 0 \\
      \mathrm{DU} & \Delta P \leq 0,\; \Delta\OI > 0 \\
      \mathrm{DD} & \Delta P \leq 0,\; \Delta\OI \leq 0
    \end{cases}
  \end{equation}
  A continuous signal assigns convergent regimes to $+1$ and
  divergent to $-1$:
  \begin{equation}
    s^{\mathrm{OI-PD}}_t = \mathbf{1}[R_t \in \{\mathrm{UU}, \mathrm{DD}\}]
                           - \mathbf{1}[R_t \in \{\mathrm{UD}, \mathrm{DU}\}]
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

OI data is sourced from the Binance FAPI \texttt{sumOpenInterest}
at 5-minute resolution, interpolated to 1-minute for alignment with
the klines dataset.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

Phase 3 tested three signal families against BTCUSDT perpetual futures
daily returns.  The key results:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Open interest is the key new information source.}  The
        OI-Price Divergence signal (P3) identifies long-liquidation
        exhaustion events via the DD regime ($\Delta P < 0$, $\Delta\OI < 0$).
        This is economically grounded: forced sellers remove supply
        and stop when all over-levered longs are cleared, leaving a
        vacuum into which prices recover.

  \item \textbf{P3 is statistically independent of N3.}  Signal
        correlation is $+0.027$.  Exclusive P3 trades achieve OOS
        Sharpe $+5.18$ ($p = 0.007$) on 57 trades that occur on days
        N3 is completely inactive.  The combined ``N3 OR P3'' strategy
        achieves $+14{,}741\bp$ OOS cumulative PnL ($+3.79$ Sharpe),
        the highest of any configuration.

  \item \textbf{VRP (P1) is not independent.}  The IC screen shows
        genuine significance at 48--72h, but the deep dive shows
        the edge collapses when N3 is excluded: P1-exclusive Sharpe
        in the DVOL $51$--$60$ zone is $-0.50$ ($p = 0.732$).  P1
        is a proxy for N3, not an orthogonal signal.

  \item \textbf{Taker flow (P2) has no daily-frequency edge.}  The
        near-constant $\TAR \approx 0.50$ confirms that the market
        is in taker equilibrium at the daily frequency.  Intraday
        taker flow may have value but is outside the current
        24h-hold framework.

  \item \textbf{DU short is killed.}  The continuation hypothesis in
        the high-DVOL regime is not supported: 8 of 9 grid cells are
        negative and the period breakdown is 5 of 6 negative.
\end{enumerate}

P3 (DD regime, DVOL $\geq \delta$) is deployed to shadow paper trading.
The 3-month forward review is due $\geq$ 14 August 2026.
